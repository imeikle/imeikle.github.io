Activity Classification

Ian Meikle

21 March 2015

Objective

One thing that people regularly do is quantify how much of a particular activity they do, but they rarely quantify how well they do it. Data was collected from accelerometers on the belt, forearm, arm, and dumbell of 6 participants. They were asked to perform barbell lifts correctly and incorrectly in 5 different ways. The objective of this study is to see if the data collected can be used to determine whether or not an exercise was performed correctly.

Data

The data was provided in two sets: training and test. The training set consisted of 19622 observations of 160 variables. The test set was much smaller, 20 observations of 160 variables. The outcome ‘classe’ was present in the training data set, but was replaced by a placeholder variable in the test set. Validation of the model was only possible by submission of predictions against the test data through a website.

Within each data set, the predictors can be classified as either raw, processed or ancillary. Raw data is recorded directly from the sensors, and consists of 9 degrees of freedom for each of the four devices. This data is processed to provide movement data {roll, pitch, yaw}, and statistics, such as mean, variance, max, etc. Ancillary data includes the name of the participant, various timestamps and time window identifiers.

Initial processing

On loading, all empty values for variables were converted to NAs. Inspection revealed that some columns contained reports of mathematical errors of the form “#DIV/0!”. These were also converted to NAs.

Too many variables were included in the supplied data to produce a generalised model. The ancillary data was immediately discarded, as the model should be able to predict the form with which the exercise is performed regardless of participant.

Processing has reduced the number of variables that need to be considered, highlighting potential influences. For example, a pairs plot of the outcome against the pitch angle of the forearm and the constituent measurements shows that it is unecessary to consider the raw data:

As a result, the raw data was removed.

Those columns which contained a very high number of NAs, and therefore very little variance, were also discarded.

Models

With the final training data set ready, a random forest model was attempted. Random forest was chosen as this problem is concerned with classification, subject to levels of continuous variables. It is an accurate method of predicting outcomes, though care needs to be taken to avoid overfitting. A separate cross-validation stage is unecessary, as bootstrapping is performed as part of the model building process. That is, for each iteration during model building, some proportion of the observations are left out and used to select between the trees built. The number of trees built per iteration also varies to allow model tuning.
